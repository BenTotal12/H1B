1. Process Overview & Eligibility

What is the H-1B Visa?

The H-1B allows U.S. employers to temporarily employ foreign workers in specialty occupations requiring a bachelor's degree in a field directly related to the job. Annual cap: 65,000 regular + 20,000 master's cap. Valid 3 years, extendable to 6 years total (exceptions for green card applicants).

Eligibility Requirements for OPT Holders

  • Valid OPT or STEM OPT status — You must currently be in valid F-1 status with an Employment Authorization Document (EAD)
  • Qualifying job offer — The offered position must be a "specialty occupation" requiring at least a bachelor's degree in a directly related field
  • Employer-employee relationship — A bona fide employer must petition on your behalf; you cannot self-petition
  • Educational qualification — You must hold at least a U.S. bachelor's degree or foreign equivalent in a field directly related to the position
  • Valid passport — Must be valid and match the information submitted in the lottery registration exactly
  • Maintained F-1 status — No unauthorized employment, no gaps in status, all SEVIS reporting up to date
💡 Key Concept: Cap-Subject vs. Cap-Exempt Most OPT holders are cap-subject (must enter lottery). Cap-exempt employers: universities, affiliated nonprofit research orgs, government research orgs—can file anytime without lottery. This guide focuses on cap-subject process.

2. Roles & Responsibilities

The H-1B process is a three-party collaboration. Understanding who does what — and when — is critical to avoiding delays, errors, or denials. The employer is the petitioner; the applicant is the beneficiary; the attorney facilitates and ensures compliance.

🏢 The Employer (Petitioner)

  • Initiates process (legal petitioner; applicant cannot self-file)
  • Registers for H-1B lottery via USCIS account
  • Pays all mandatory fees: base, ACWIA, fraud prevention, asylum program
  • Defines job duties and degree requirements for specialty occupation
  • Obtains prevailing wage determination (DOL or OES data)
  • Files Labor Condition Application (LCA) with DOL
  • Maintains public access file at worksite
  • Posts LCA notice at worksite for 10 business days
  • Signs and files Form I-129 with supporting evidence
  • Provides detailed employer support letter
  • Pays higher of prevailing/actual wage from day one
  • Cannot retaliate against worker for asserting H-1B rights

⚖ The Immigration Attorney

  • Evaluates specialty occupation eligibility and applicant qualifications
  • Determines prevailing wage strategy (SOC code, wage level, geographic area)—critical for weighted selection
  • Prepares and files LCA (Form ETA-9035) with DOL
  • Submits lottery registration through USCIS online account
  • Prepares complete I-129 petition package with supporting docs
  • Drafts employer support letter with specialty occupation language
  • Obtains credential evaluations for foreign degrees or experience equivalency
  • Coordinates change of status strategy (COS vs. consular processing)
  • Responds to RFEs within 30-87 day deadline
  • Advises on cap-gap protections and status maintenance
  • Monitors case and communicates updates to employer and applicant
  • Ensures compliance with LCA posting and public access file requirements

👤 The Applicant (Beneficiary)

  • Provides all documents: passport, I-94, visa stamps, I-20s, EAD, diplomas, transcripts, resume
  • Ensures passport info matches exactly—verify number, country, expiration carefully
  • Obtains credential evaluation for foreign degrees (NACES/AICE member evaluator)
  • Maintains valid F-1/OPT status continuously—lapses or violations can be fatal
  • Reports employment to DSO per OPT/STEM OPT requirements (including semi-annual STEM validation)
  • Does NOT pay employer fees (base, training, fraud, asylum)—may pay premium processing only
  • Reviews all documents for accuracy before submission
  • Responds promptly to attorney requests—delays cause missed deadlines
  • Avoids international travel without attorney guidance (can terminate cap-gap/COS)
  • Maintains organized personal immigration file with copies of all documents
  • Reports job/address changes immediately; STEM OPT to DSO within 10 days

🚫 Common Role-Related Denial Risks

  • Applicant pays filing fees: Beneficiary paying employer-obligated fees (directly or via payroll deduction) = denial/revocation and employer debarment
  • No employer-employee relationship: Must prove right to control work (hire, fire, supervise, assign duties)—heightened scrutiny for third-party placements
  • Cannot pay prevailing wage: Employer must demonstrate financial ability via tax returns, financial statements, or annual reports

4. H-1B Lottery Registration (March 2026)

Step-by-Step Registration Process

Step Who Action Critical Details
1 Employer / Attorney Create USCIS online account Employer creates an organizational account at myaccount.uscis.gov. Attorney can be added as an authorized representative. Do this well before March 4.
2 Attorney Enter registrant (employer) info Company name, EIN, address, NAICS code, number of employees, authorized signatory details. Must exactly match IRS records.
3 Attorney Enter beneficiary information Full legal name (as on passport), DOB, country of birth, citizenship, gender, passport number, passport expiration, passport issuing country, master's cap eligibility.
4 Employer Review and sign attestation Under penalty of perjury: information is true, job offer is legitimate, no coordination with other registrants to unfairly increase selection chances.
5 Employer / Attorney Pay registration fee $215 per beneficiary via pay.gov. Payment must clear — failed payments completely invalidate the registration. Verify the credit card before submission.
6 Employer / Attorney Submit registration Carefully confirm all details before final submission. Registrations may be edited or deleted until the window closes on March 19 at noon ET.

🚫 Registration Pitfalls That Will Disqualify Your Entry

  • Invalid passport information: Entering "N/A," "000000," or incorrect passport details causes the registration to be deemed not properly submitted — it won't be included in the lottery at all
  • Duplicate registrations by same employer: Only one registration per beneficiary per employer. Submitting duplicates can flag your case for fraud review
  • Failed payment: If the credit card payment fails or is reversed, the registration is permanently excluded from the lottery
  • Coordinated multiple registrations: Working with other companies to submit additional registrations for the same beneficiary to increase odds is federal fraud. USCIS actively investigates, denies petitions, and refers cases for criminal prosecution
  • Wrong passport: Registering with a passport you will NOT use for U.S. entry can invalidate the registration
  • Missing the window: The window closes at noon ET on March 19 — not midnight. Registrations submitted even one minute late are rejected

If You Are Selected in the Lottery

6. Approval & Transitioning to H-1B Status

Change of Status (COS) — Most Common for OPT Holders in the U.S.

If COS approved, H-1B starts Oct 1, 2026. You receive Form I-797A with new I-94 attached.

  • Cannot begin H-1B employment before Oct 1 even if approved earlier—remain on F-1/OPT until then
  • Continue working under OPT between approval and Oct 1
  • F-1 terminates Oct 1—notify DSO and close SEVIS
  • No visa stamp needed to work in U.S.—I-797A with I-94 sufficient while in country
  • Travel abroad after Oct 1 requires H-1B visa stamp from consulate before re-entry
  • First H-1B valid 3 years; extendable 3 more (6 years total, exceptions for green card applicants)

Consular Processing — If You Are Outside the U.S.

If you chose consular processing or left the U.S.:

  • Wait for I-797B (Approval Notice for consular processing) from attorney
  • Complete Form DS-160 (Online Nonimmigrant Visa Application)
  • Schedule visa interview at U.S. consulate/embassy
  • Bring: I-797B, passport, DS-160 confirmation, photo, support letter, diplomas, transcripts
  • Consular officer may ask detailed questions about job, employer, duties, qualifications
  • Upon visa issuance, may enter U.S. up to 10 days before Oct 1
💡 COS vs. Consular Processing — Which to Choose? For OPT holders in U.S., COS is almost always preferred. Avoids consular interview, visa denial risk, and administrative processing delays. Consular processing necessary if: (a) travel before COS approved; (b) prior visa issues need consulate resolution; (c) prefer visiting family abroad and obtaining visa stamp immediately. Discuss with attorney.

7. OPT Cap-Gap Extension — Critical for OPT Holders

✔ What is the Cap-Gap? Cap-gap is automatic extension of F-1 status and (in some cases) OPT/STEM OPT work authorization for beneficiaries of timely filed H-1B cap petitions with COS request. Bridges gap between OPT end and Oct 1 H-1B start—prevents status/work authorization lapse.

How It Works

Scenario Cap-Gap Extension Work Authorization?
H-1B petition filed with COS request while OPT EAD is still valid F-1 status AND employment authorization automatically extend through September 30 YES — continue working under OPT through September 30
H-1B petition filed during the 60-day grace period after OPT expires (no active EAD) F-1 status extends through September 30 NO — you may remain in the U.S. legally but cannot work until October 1 H-1B start
H-1B petition is denied or revoked before October 1 Cap-gap extension terminates immediately NO — you revert to regular F-1 grace period rules; depart or pursue alternatives
H-1B petition is withdrawn before October 1 Cap-gap extension terminates immediately NO
Beneficiary travels outside the U.S. while on cap-gap Cap-gap extension terminates upon departure NO — may not be able to return until H-1B visa is issued by a consulate

🚫 Cap-Gap Risks — Read Carefully

  • Lottery selection alone is NOT enough: The cap-gap does NOT begin until the actual I-129 petition with COS request is filed and received by USCIS. Being selected in the lottery only gives you the right to file. If your OPT expires before the petition is filed, you enter the 60-day grace period (no work authorization).
  • Travel kills the cap-gap: Leaving the U.S. while on cap-gap extension terminates the extension. You may not be able to return until your H-1B visa is issued at a consulate. Do not travel internationally during this period without explicit attorney guidance.
  • OPT expires before petition filed: If the employer delays filing and your OPT expires before the I-129 is filed, you lose work authorization during the gap between OPT expiration and filing. File as early as possible within the window.
  • STEM OPT reporting failures: Missing your STEM OPT semi-annual validation reporting to your DSO, or failing to report employment changes within 10 days, can result in OPT termination — which can destroy the cap-gap protection.
  • Unauthorized employment: Any employment outside the scope of your OPT authorization (wrong employer, too many hours, etc.) can terminate your F-1 status and by extension the cap-gap.

8. New Weighted Selection Process (Effective FY 2027)

DHS final rule (effective Feb 27, 2026) replaces random lottery with weighted selection favoring higher-wage positions.

How the Weighting Works

  • Each registration weighted by offered wage relative to prevailing wage for occupation and geographic area
  • Higher wages = greater weight = higher selection probability
  • Still includes random element—NOT purely ranked by salary. Lower wages still have chance (reduced vs. old system)
  • Master's cap (20,000 for U.S. advanced degrees) maintained separately
  • Beneficiary-centric selection (one selection per person regardless of employer count) remains

What This Means for OPT Applicants

  • Entry-level wages (Level I): Lower selection odds vs. old random lottery
  • Specialized skills, advanced degrees, experience: Higher wages = significant advantage
  • Employers may offer higher compensation to improve selection chances
  • SOC code and geographic area are strategically critical: Same salary = different wage levels in different markets. Example—SOC 11-1021.00 General and Operations Managers in San Bernardino County: $96K = Level II, but same $96K might be Level I in San Francisco Bay Area (see Section 4 for full wage breakdown)
  • Does not affect cap-exempt employers (universities, etc.)
  • Work with attorney to honestly optimize wage level classification
⚠ Do NOT Misrepresent Wage Levels or Job Duties Inflating wage level, misclassifying SOC code, or misrepresenting duties = fraud. Results: petition denial, revocation, permanent immigration bars (INA 212(a)(6)(C)), criminal prosecution. USCIS cross-references offered position, duties, and qualifications against claimed wage level. Integrity measures from FY 2024–2026 continue with aggressive enforcement.

9. Top Denial Risks & How to Avoid Them

Common RFE and denial reasons with prevention strategies. Address proactively during petition prep.

# Denial Risk What Goes Wrong How to Prevent It
1 Not a Specialty Occupation USCIS determines the position does not require a specific bachelor's degree. Broad titles like "Business Analyst" or "IT Specialist" with generic degree requirements are frequently challenged. The support letter must show specific duties requiring specialized knowledge. Prove: (a) a degree in a specific field is required, (b) this is the industry norm (cite O*NET, BLS data), (c) the employer has a history of requiring this degree, (d) duties are complex enough that only a degreed person could perform them. Include expert opinion letters from professors or industry experts if needed.
2 Beneficiary Not Qualified The degree does not directly relate to the specialty occupation. A degree in "Liberal Arts" for a software developer role, or a 3-year foreign degree without proper equivalency evaluation. Obtain a thorough credential evaluation from a NACES or AICE-member evaluation service. If relying on work experience (3 years of progressive experience = 1 year of education), get detailed experience letters on company letterhead with specific duties, skills used, and dates of employment. Ensure the degree-to-job match is clearly articulated.
3 Employer-Employee Relationship Staffing companies, IT consulting firms, or employers placing workers at third-party client sites fail to demonstrate sufficient control over the beneficiary's daily work. Provide: executed contracts/MSAs/SOWs with end-clients, detailed organizational charts, supervision structure, description of the right to hire/fire/pay/supervise/assign duties. If at a third-party site, include an end-client letter confirming the engagement, site, duration, and reporting structure. Provide a detailed itinerary of services.
4 Inability to Pay Prevailing Wage Employer cannot demonstrate financial ability to pay the wage through tax returns, financials, or other evidence. Small companies and startups are particularly vulnerable. Provide: most recent year's federal tax return, audited financial statements, annual report (public companies), or bank statements showing sufficient net income or net current assets. For startups: include funding documentation, investor term sheets, bank statements, and a credible business plan.
5 LCA Deficiencies LCA not certified before filing. Worksite address doesn't match the petition. Wage on LCA is below prevailing wage. SOC code doesn't match the actual job duties. File the LCA early (February/March). Triple-check that the worksite address, SOC code, wage level, and dates are 100% consistent across the LCA, I-129, and support letter. If there are multiple worksites, each must be listed on the LCA or a separate LCA filed.
6 Status Maintenance Issues Gap in F-1 status, unauthorized employment, failure to report to DSO during STEM OPT, missed SEVIS reporting, or 60-day grace period violations. Review your SEVIS record with your DSO. Ensure all employment has been properly reported and validated. Keep every I-20, EAD card, and status document. Address any gaps or issues with your attorney before filing. Maintain full compliance throughout.
7 Fraud / Misrepresentation False information on registration or petition. "Benching" (not paying the H-1B wage during non-productive time). Fictitious or speculative job offers. Sham employers. USCIS conducts site visits (FDNS). Ensure the job is real, the employer is genuine, and ALL information is 100% truthful. A single material misrepresentation can trigger a permanent immigration bar under INA 212(a)(6)(C). Benching violations can lead to back-wage claims and employer debarment.
8 Missed Deadlines Petition not filed within the filing window. RFE response submitted late. LCA not posted for the required 10 consecutive business days. Registration fee payment failed. Track ALL deadlines meticulously — use multiple calendar reminders with buffer days. Premium processing helps ensure timely adjudication. Verify fee payments cleared. Confirm LCA posting dates with HR. Never wait until the last day for anything.

12. Frequently Asked Questions

Can I travel while my H-1B petition is pending?

It depends on your current status and the type of petition filed:

  • Change of Status (COS): Traveling while a COS is pending is considered "abandonment" of the COS request. The H-1B may still be approved for consular processing, but you will have to leave the U.S. and likely get a visa stamp. Do not travel without attorney approval.
  • Consular Processing: You can travel, but you must have a valid visa to re-enter. You cannot enter in H-1B status until 10 days before Oct 1 with a valid H-1B visa.
  • Cap-Gap: Traveling terminates the cap-gap extension. You cannot return until the H-1B is approved and you have the visa stamp.
Who pays for the H-1B fees?

The employer must pay the registration fee, base filing fee, ACWIA training fee, fraud prevention fee, and asylum program fee. Requiring the employee to pay these (even via payroll deduction) is illegal. The employee (beneficiary) generally pays for their own credential evaluations, visa application fees (at the consulate), and may voluntarily pay the premium processing fee.

What if I get laid off while on H-1B?

You have a 60-day grace period (or until the end of your I-94, whichever is shorter) to find a new employer to file an H-1B transfer petition, change to another status (like H-4 or F-1), or depart the U.S.

Can my spouse work?

In general, H-4 spouses are not authorized to work unless the H-1B holder has an approved I-140 (immigrant petition for green card). This typically takes several years.
